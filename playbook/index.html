Blog Writing

So what does it take to generate a LOT of traffic with blogging?

Through my research I found that there were a number of similarities between the most popular blogs on the Internet, and the following are my rules to create ‘truly epic’ blog posts:

  • Write long posts between 2500 - 3000 words. The average content length for a web page that ranks in the top 10 results for any keyword on Google has over 2,000 words.
  • Posts with videos, images, and lists will attract over 40% more links than a plain text post. (Don’t worry about sourcing images—I’ll do that.)
  • Back up all claims made with links to sources to prove authenticity.
  • Start each post with a story. It doesn’t have to be your story—use someone else’s story or make one up. James Clear does this really well, as well as Pat.
  • Use a friendly, chatty, informal tone.
  • Be opinionated: it might be your own opinion, or my opinion that we’ve agreed you’ll take for that particular post. When comparing two products/services/tools, always state which one you like best and explain why.
  • Your post will explain WHAT or WHY to do something, or give instructions of HOW to do it step by step.
  • Go into the details, don’t just skim the surface like 99% of all other blog posts out there.

Tips on structure and layout

People who read blogs tend to quickly scan the article first, to see if it is something they want to read. As a result, large blocks of text do not do well as blog posts.

Instead, you will need to make use of:

  • A strong opening paragraph that compels the reader to want to keep reading (the opening “sells” the remainder of the post).
  • An opening paragraph will begin with a story or 2-3 questions that set the stage for the post.
  • Paragraphs with a maximum of 3 sentences each.
  • A headline or sub-headline after a maximum of 3 paragraphs. Headlines should be clear and compelling, as they do the job of telling the story of the post: the reader should be able to skim the headlines and get a sense of what you will be writing about.
  • Bullet points or numbered lists.

Sales Writing

Forms

When a form is required, for example on a landing page, make sure to write the copy for:

  • The form title if it needs one.
  • Intro blurb, again if it needs one.
  • Form field names.
  • The button copy—important this is a strong call-to-action.

The form copy is often an afterthought which it shouldn’t be as it’s generally the main call-to-action on the page.
